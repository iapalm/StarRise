Bortle scale

The Bortle scale measures the degree to which the night sky in an area is affected by light pollution. Higher values on the Bortle scale correlate to brighter night skies and worse astronomical conditons. The scale was created in 2001 by John E. Bortle to help astronomers determine the darkness of an observing point. Use the table below to determine your area's Bortle rating, with 1 being the darkest night skies on Earth and 9 being the brightest night skies.


Bortle rating Description Naked Eye Limiting Magnitude (NELM) Visibility
1 Darkest night sky - no apparent light pollution 7.5 - 8.0
  • Many Messier objects and globular clusters are visible to the naked eye and appear as fuzzy stars
  • The gegenschien is visible
  • Zodiacal light is visible and colorful
  • Zodiacal band is visible
  • Portions of the Milky Way cast shadows
  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is visible to the naked eye

  • Zodiacal light is visible and color is visible
  • Light pollution apparent on horizon
  • Clouds are lit only on horizon

  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is visible to the naked eye

  • Light pollution apparent on horizon in several directions
  • Clouds are lit on horizon
  • Milky way visible
  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is visible only with averted vision and when high in the sky

  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is invisible

  • Clouds are lit across the sky
  • Milky way slightly visible only near zenith
  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is invisible
  • Surroundings easily visible

  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is invisible

  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is invisible

  • Zodiacal light completely invisible

  • Milky way completely invisible
  • M33 (Triangulum galaxy) is invisible
  • Surroundings easily visible - you could read small print
