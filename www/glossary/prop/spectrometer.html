Astronomical spectroscopy

Astronomical spectroscopy is the practice of measuring the spectrum of electromagnetic radiation emitted from a star. Spectroscopy can be used to deduce a star's chemical compisition, temperature, density, and motion (including rotation and movement in space relative to Earth).

Spectrometers spread light emitted from the object of interest into its wavelengths creating a spectrum. Within the spectrum are two important clues as to the star's properties: emission and absorbtion lines. Emission lines are created when an electron leaves the excited state and emits a photon, which is observed in the spectrum. Absorbtion lines are created when an electron enters the excited state, which occurs when an electron absorbs energy. An element's orbital compositon determines which wavelengths are absorbed. Because each element has a unique orbital compositon, each element has a unique absorbtion spectrum.



Now compare those emission and absorption spectrums with the sun's absorption spectrum. The sun is primarily composed of hydrogen.




Notice how the absorption lines of hydrogen are visible in the sun's absorption spectrum. The other absorption lines are caused by other elements, like helium. Basically, this is how astronomers determine the chemical composition of distant stars - using just light!
